# Final Project 

To extend what you've learned in this course, you will complete a final project which will be presented to the class during the final exam period. Grads and undergrads have slightly different requirements, so please make sure that you read this document closely. 

## Goals
The LLM landscape is extremely complex and changing constantly, and it's just not possible to cover everything in one semester. This course has been designed to give you a foundation to dig deeper into specific sub-areas that interest you. In the final project, you will perform some targeted, specific research into a topic related to LLMs. Instead of a final exam, you will present the results of your research to the whole class during the final session. In doing so, you should:

- Apply what you've learned this semester in an independent research setting, 
- Gain experience synthesizing technical information into a clear, informative presentation, and 
- Enrich your classmates' with up-to-date information that was not already covered in the course. 

## Basic requirements
These basic requirements are the same for both undergrads and graduates. If you have any questions or do not understand these requirements, please reach out to me well in advance of the deadline! You should start thinking about the final project *now*, not a few days before it is due. 

1. You may work in groups of up to three students. Groups must be either all grad or all undergrad (no mixed grad/undergrad groups).
2. Your topic should not be something that has been directly covered already in the course. It should *extend* existing course material, not repeat it. An example of an inappropriate topic would be "Supervised Fine Tuning". An appropriate topic might be "Scaling Laws", or "Model Context Protocol".
3. Because we want the final exam session to be informative for everyone, we don't want multiple groups to present on the same topic. There will be a sign up sheet for groups and topics available after Spring Break. You will be able to change your topic, but we want to make sure that we avoid duplicate presentations as much as possible.  

### Undergrad requirements
1. The presentation should be between 7 and 10 minutes in length. 
2. You should have slides that are clear, visually appealing, and help your audience understand your topic better. 
3. If you are working in a group, each group member should speak for approximately an equal amount of time.
4. All sources (academic papers, blog posts, websites, models...) *must* be cited in your slides. You will be heavily penalized for failing to cite sources. However, you are strongly encouraged to reference other people's work with the appropriate attribution!
5. Before the date of the presentation, you will be required to submit a PDF copy of your slides via CMS.

### Grad requirements
Graduate students are responsible for all of the above undergraduate requirements. In addition, they will also be required to do the following:
#### Topic
Graduates must pick from the following types of presentations:
- **Literature review:** An organized and thoughtful summary of academic research in a specific sub-area.
- **Experiment:** You conduct an original experiment related to or using LLMs and report your results.
- **Application:** You present your designs for or prototype of an application that is related to LLMs. Your application doesn't have to be entirely functional, but it needs to be technically plausible. If the application relies on LLMs, you should clearly articulate how.

#### Writeup
In addition to your presentation, grads will submit a written report on your project using a provided $\LaTeX$ template. Your written report should be at minimum two pages, including formatted references, figures, and diagrams, as appropriate.
